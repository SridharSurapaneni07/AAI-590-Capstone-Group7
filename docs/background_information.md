# Background Information

**1. What other methods/technologies exist that attempt to solve the same problem/answer the same question that your project will address?**

Historically, real estate valuation models have relied on traditional statistical techniques like Hedonic Pricing Models (HPM). HPMs operate by treating a property as a bundle of individual features (e.g., number of bedrooms, square footage, distance to city center) and using basic linear regression to estimate the price contribution of each feature. 

More recently, tree-based ensemble methods—specifically Random Forest and XGBoost (Extreme Gradient Boosting) **(Chen & Guestrin, 2016)**—have become the industry standard for commercial Automated Valuation Models (AVMs). These algorithms iteratively build "decision trees" to handle complex, non-linear relationships and outliers in housing data. In the present market, leading US commercial competitors like Zillow (with its "Zestimate") and Redfin, alongside Indian equivalents such as MagicBricks, 99acres, and Housing.com, still predominantly rely on these tabular machine learning models. 

While easier to implement, scale, and maintain, these tabular-first models suffer from a major limitation: they require highly structured data. They generally ignore unstructured data like nuanced property descriptions, cultural layout indicators, and high-resolution images, which arguably play the largest role in actual human buyer decisions. When competitors do utilize images, they frequently use older Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) **(LeCun et al., 1998)** in a simplistic manner—for example, performing basic Image Tagging to just classify a room as a "kitchen" or "bathroom"—rather than dynamically extracting latent "aesthetic value" and mathematically fusing it with textual data via cross-attention.

**2. How was your machine learning method/system developed, and what are its benefits (especially those relevant to your project)?**

Our project, **BharatPropAdvisor**, deviates from simpler AVM paradigms by employing a novel, end-to-end **Multimodal Fusion Architecture**. The system was developed by creating three distinct neural network branches to simultaneously process diverse data streams, mirroring how a human processes multiple senses when viewing a property:

*   **Tabular Branch (Feed-Forward Neural Network):** 
    *   *Technical Background:* Traditional algorithms like XGBoost **(Chen & Guestrin, 2016)** cannot be seamlessly integrated into deep learning architectures because they are not differentiable (i.e., you cannot use backpropagation to train them alongside image and text data). 
    *   *Why We Chose It:* We replaced the traditional baseline with a Feed-Forward Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP). This network processes numerical and categorical features (e.g., Area, Bathrooms, Amenities) into dense mathematical vectors (embeddings), allowing the tabular data to map its geometric relationships directly alongside visual and textual vectors.
*   **Vision Branch (Vision Transformer - ViT):** 
    *   *Technical Background:* While traditional CNNs look at local pixel patches (like edges and textures), Vision Transformers (`ViT-Base-Patch16-224`) **(Dosovitskiy et al., 2020)** treat an image as a sequence of patches—much like reading words in a sentence. This allows the model to utilize "Self-Attention" mechanisms to understand global context and lighting dynamics across an entire room simultaneously.
    *   *Why We Chose It:* We fine-tuned a pre-trained ViT as a continuous regressor to evaluate property images and extract a latent "Premium Aesthetic Score." We chose a Transformer over a CNN because our goal is not localized tagging (e.g., finding a sink), but rather holistic aesthetic quantification—a crucial driver of property premiums.
*   **Text Branch (Transformer-based NLP - BERT):** 
    *   *Technical Background:* BERT (`bert-base-multilingual-cased`) **(Devlin et al., 2019)** reads text bidirectionally to understand the deep semantic context of words within a sentence, far surpassing older methods like TF-IDF or simple keyword matching.
    *   *Why We Chose It:* Property descriptions contain highly subjective cultural indicators. We leverage a multilingual BERT model initially heavily focused on deeply understanding **Indian regional languages** to parse unstructured listings for highly localized factors like *Vastu Shastra* (the traditional Indian system of architecture) compliance, which simpler tabular models fundamentally cannot grasp. Crucially, the architecture is inherently scalable; once verified on Indian native languages, this exact NLP branch layout will be expanded seamlessly to other regional and classical languages, including **Spanish, French, and Latin**.

**The Fusion Layer & The "Bharat" Benefit:**
These three independent embeddings are treated as a sequence and passed through a custom Transformer Encoder with "Cross-Attention" **(Vaswani et al., 2017)**, allowing the system to understand the relationship *between* the visual beauty, the text description, and the physical metrics simultaneously. 

The primary benefit of this system is its holistic approach, **specifically tailored and focused on localized language markets, starting with Indian languages**. Unlike existing state-of-the-art models (like Zillow’s Zestimate) which are primarily trained on US housing data and thus optimized strictly for English linguistics and Western demographics, our model captures the unique nuances of "Bharat." By explicitly modeling Indian regional textual data, cultural concepts, and architectural rules, our system more accurately mimics how a native buyer operates. Furthermore, this foundation is robust: transitioning from processing Indian market dialects into other global markets (e.g., Spanish and French) is functionally native to the multilingual pipeline, allowing our project to scale intelligently into diverse geographies where US-trained monolingual models fail.

**3. What existing work demonstrates that these method(s) are appropriate (or particularly well-suited) to use in your project?**

Recent academic literature on "Multimodal Fusion Learning" demonstrates that cross-attention mechanisms **(Vaswani et al., 2017)**—where representations from one modality (e.g., images) attend to another (e.g., text or tabular features)—yield highly robust predictive models that significantly outperform simple concatenation baselines. 

Vision Transformers (ViTs) **(Dosovitskiy et al., 2020)** have empirically been shown to outperform traditional CNNs on complex classification and regression tasks by efficiently learning global context (the overall aesthetic feel of a space). Furthermore, transformer-based NLP models like BERT **(Devlin et al., 2019)** are the established state-of-the-art in natural language processing for extracting nuanced, subjective sentiment from multilingual unstructured text. Leveraging these advanced deep learning paradigms together ensures the model can accurately quantify the aesthetic and cultural variables that traditional tabular-only AVMs notoriously miss.

**4. What are some examples of similar projects that use the method(s) that you have chosen?**

*   **Commercial Projects:** While most Indian PropTech platforms (e.g., MagicBricks, Housing.com) still rely on easier, tabular-only prediction models, global PropTech giants like Zillow and Redfin are increasingly augmenting their pipelines with multimodal techniques. For example, Zillow has publicly discussed utilizing computer vision on property photos to estimate home condition to adjust their Zestimate algorithm. However, even these global implementations are strictly optimized for US/Western geographies and pure-English datasets. Our project adopts a similarly cutting-edge multimodal structure but secures a massive advantage by rooting itself in local linguistic capabilities (Indian languages) initially, with a pre-built capability to conquer non-English/multi-lingual markets globally (Spanish, French, Latin dialects) faster than a monolithic legacy system could.
*   **Academic Research:** Recent academic papers in "Multimodal Learning for Real Estate Valuation" demonstrate theoretical architectures that combine image features with tabular metadata to predict housing prices. Cutting-edge research specifically highlights the efficacy of using transformer-based cross-attention layers **(Vaswani et al., 2017)** to fuse text descriptions, spatial metadata, and image embeddings. While academic researchers have validated this architecture conceptually, complex natively-multilingual, end-to-end systems remain nascent in commercial PropTech due to the intense computational overhead and difficulty to implement compared to traditional models.
